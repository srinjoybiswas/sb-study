Unit I · 6 Hours

Demographic Profile of India

📊 1.1 What is Demography?

📖 Definition Demography is the scientific study of human populations — how large they are, how they grow or shrink, who lives in them, and how they change over time. The word comes from the Greek words demos (people) and graphein (to write).

Think of it like a "report card" of a country's people. Just like a hospital monitors a patient's health with numbers (blood pressure, temperature, weight), a country monitors its population with numbers like birth rate, death rate, literacy rate, and so on.

India is the most populous country in the world (surpassing China in 2023) with over 1.44 billion people. Understanding its demography is essential for planning schools, hospitals, jobs, roads, and food supply.

📈 1.2 Population Growth in India

India's population has grown enormously over the past 200 years. In 1901, India had about 238 million people. By 2011, it had crossed 1.2 billion. This rapid growth is called a population explosion.

Key Concepts in Population Growth

🐣 Birth Rate (BR)

Number of live births per 1,000 people per year. Higher BR = faster population growth. India's BR has fallen from 41.7 (1951) to about 19.5 (2020).

💀 Death Rate (DR)

Number of deaths per 1,000 people per year. India's DR fell sharply due to better medicine and sanitation — from 27.4 (1951) to about 7.4 (2020).

📊 Natural Growth Rate

NR = BR − DR. When births are much more than deaths, population grows fast. India's NR was very high in the 1970s-80s.

👶 Infant Mortality Rate

Deaths of children under 1 year per 1,000 live births. India's IMR fell from 146 (1951) to about 28 (2020) — a huge achievement.

Phases of Population Growth in India

1
Phase 1: 1901–1921 (Stagnant Growth)

Very slow growth. High birth rates but also very high death rates due to famines, epidemics (plague, influenza), and poor healthcare. This period is called the "Era of Great Divide".

2
Phase 2: 1921–1951 (Steady Growth)

Population began to grow steadily. Death rates fell due to better sanitation and public health programs. Birth rates remained high. 1921 is called the "Year of Great Divide" (turning point).

3
Phase 3: 1951–1981 (Population Explosion)

Fastest growth phase. Death rates fell sharply with independence, new hospitals, medicines (penicillin, vaccines), and the Green Revolution ensured food. But birth rates stayed high = population explosion.

4
Phase 4: 1981–Present (Declining Growth Rate)

Growth rate is slowing. Better education (especially for women), family planning programs, later marriages, and urbanisation are reducing birth rates. However, total population is still large.

💡 Key Fact Demographic Transition Theory explains that as societies develop economically, they move from high birth + high death rates → high birth + low death rates → low birth + low death rates. India is currently in the third stage of this transition.

Factors Causing High Population Growth in India

  • Early marriage – Girls marry young, leading to more years of childbearing
  • Son preference – Families keep having children hoping for a son
  • Low education – Less educated women tend to have more children
  • Lack of family planning awareness – Especially in rural areas
  • Religious beliefs – Some communities oppose birth control
  • Poverty – Children are seen as extra hands to earn income
  • Joint family system – Many people share costs, reducing fear of more children

India has a very young population — this is called a "young age structure". About 65% of Indians are below 35 years of age. This is called India's "demographic dividend" — a large working-age population that can drive economic growth IF they are educated and employed.

Language in India

India has no single national language but Hindi is the official language of the Union Government. India has 22 scheduled languages listed in the 8th Schedule of the Constitution. In reality, India has over 1,600 dialects and languages.

  • Hindi – Spoken by ~44% as first language; used across North India
  • Bengali – Second most spoken; West Bengal and Bangladesh
  • Telugu, Tamil, Kannada, Malayalam – Major Dravidian languages of South India
  • Marathi, Gujarati, Punjabi, Odia – Major regional languages
  • English – Associate official language; used in higher education, courts, and business
💡 Important Concept Linguistic diversity is both India's strength (rich cultural heritage) and a challenge (communication barriers, regional identities can cause tensions). The States Reorganisation Act of 1956 reorganised states largely on linguistic lines to address this.

🏗️ 1.4 Occupations in India

India's working population is engaged in three main sectors. The shift of workers from agriculture to industry and services is a sign of economic development.

🌾 Primary Sector

Agriculture, fishing, forestry, mining. About 45–50% of workers are in this sector, though it contributes only ~18% to GDP. Major issue: disguised unemployment.

🏭 Secondary Sector

Manufacturing, construction, industries. About 25% of workers. Growing sector with Special Economic Zones (SEZs), Make in India initiative.

💼 Tertiary Sector

IT, banking, healthcare, education, trade, transport. Fastest growing sector; contributes about 55% of GDP. India is a global IT and services hub.

⚠️ Key Problem Disguised Unemployment — In agriculture, more people work on a farm than actually needed. If 5 people work on land where 2 are sufficient, 3 are "disguisedly unemployed". Their removal would NOT reduce output. This is a major Indian problem.

🏛️ 1.5 National Population Policy (NPP 2000)

India launched its National Population Policy in 2000. It was the first comprehensive policy to address population issues holistically — not just about having fewer children, but about health, education, and rights.

Three Main Goals of NPP 2000

  1. Immediate Goal – Meet unmet needs for contraception, healthcare, and reduce IMR and MMR (maternal mortality rate)
  2. Medium-term Goal – Bring Total Fertility Rate (TFR) to replacement level (2.1 children per woman) by 2010
  3. Long-term Goal – Achieve stable population by 2045, consistent with sustainable economic growth

Key Features of NPP 2000

  • Free and compulsory education up to age 14
  • Reduce infant mortality rate below 30 per 1,000 live births
  • Universal immunisation of children
  • Promote delayed marriage (girls ≥ 18, boys ≥ 21)
  • Incentives for adopting small family norms
  • Integrate family welfare into poverty alleviation and development programs
Unit II · 6 Hours

Indian Society & Culture

🏘️ 2.1 What is Society?

📖 Definition Society is a group of people who live together in a specific geographic area, share common norms, values, and institutions, and interact with each other on a regular basis. Society is NOT just a collection of people — it involves relationships, rules, roles, and shared identity.

Think of society like a school. It has teachers, students, a principal — each has a role. There are rules (wear uniform, don't bully), shared values (respect, learning), and everyone interacts. Together they form a "school society".

"Man is by nature a social animal" — Aristotle

Types of Society (Based on Structure)

👨‍👩‍👧 Tribal Society

Organized around kinship and clans. Strong oral traditions, common land, own language/dialect. India has over 700 tribal communities (Adivasis).

🏡 Rural Society

Agriculture-based; strong family ties, caste system is important, joint family common, less occupational diversity, conservative values.

🏙️ Urban Society

Industry/service based; nuclear families, more individualism, less caste consciousness, more social mobility, faster change.

🌐 Modern/Global Society

Driven by technology, globalisation, internet. High mobility, multicultural exposure, blurring of traditional boundaries.

Basic Characteristics of Indian Society

  • Unity in Diversity – Despite hundreds of languages, castes, and religions, Indians share a common cultural identity and nationhood
  • Family-centric – The family (especially joint family) is the basic unit of Indian society
  • Caste System – Hierarchical social structure based on birth (discussed in Unit III)
  • Religious pluralism – Multiple faiths coexist, influencing laws, customs, and daily life
  • Patriarchal system – Male dominance in most regions, though changing
  • Rural dominance – Even today, ~65% of Indians live in villages
  • Sacred-secular mix – Religion is deeply embedded in social, political, and economic life

🎭 2.2 Culture — Meaning, Features & Diversity

📖 Definition Culture is the total way of life of a group of people. It includes their beliefs, values, customs, language, art, music, food, dress, laws, morals, and everything learned and shared within a community. Culture is not inherited biologically — it is learned (this is called "enculturation").

Simple analogy: If society is a river, culture is the water flowing in it. Culture shapes how we think, behave, and understand the world around us.

Components of Culture

📜 Material Culture

Physical, tangible objects created by humans — tools, buildings, clothes, food, technology, art. E.g., Indian temples, saris, tabla, biryani.

💭 Non-Material Culture

Intangible elements — values, beliefs, norms, laws, language, religion, customs, folklore. E.g., Ahimsa (non-violence), respect for elders, Namaste greeting.

📏 Norms

Rules of acceptable behaviour. Formal norms (laws) and informal norms (customs, etiquette). E.g., removing shoes before entering a temple.

❤️ Values

Shared beliefs about what is good, right, and desirable. E.g., dharma, karma, family loyalty, respect for teachers (Guru Dakshina).

Key Features / Characteristics of Culture

  1. Culture is learned – No one is born with culture; it is acquired through socialisation (family, school, religion, media)
  2. Culture is shared – It is a collective property, not individual. You speak a language only because others do too
  3. Culture is transmitted – Passed from one generation to next through language, education, and tradition
  4. Culture is dynamic – It changes over time. Indian culture has changed with Mughal influence, British rule, globalisation, internet
  5. Culture is cumulative – Each generation adds to what it inherits; knowledge builds up over time
  6. Culture is symbolic – Much of culture is communicated through symbols: the Tricolour, Om, Cross, Crescent Moon
  7. Culture is diverse – Different groups have different cultures (subcultures) based on region, religion, tribe, class
  8. Culture is adaptive – Cultures adapt to their environment. Kashmiris wear warm woollen clothes; Rajasthanis wear light cotton due to climate
  9. Culture integrates society – Common culture creates a sense of "we-feeling" and keeps society together

Cultural Diversity of India

India's cultural diversity is unmatched in the world. The same country has snow-capped mountain tribes and tropical coastal fishing communities, ancient Vedic traditions and modern tech-savvy urban youth. This diversity comes from:

🌏 Geographic Diversity

Mountains, plains, deserts, coasts — each creates different lifestyles, foods, and customs. Punjab's bhangra vs Kerala's Kathakali.

📜 Historical Diversity

Successive invasions (Aryans, Mughals, British) each left cultural imprints — architecture, language, food, clothing.

🕉️ Religious Diversity

Every major world religion is practiced in India. Each brings its own festivals, food rules, dress, and rituals.

🗣️ Linguistic Diversity

22 scheduled languages, 1600+ dialects. Each has its own rich literature, music, and oral traditions.

🔑 Remember India's motto is "Unity in Diversity" — despite being extraordinarily diverse, Indians share common values of dharma, family, hospitality, and spiritual seeking. Dr. B.R. Ambedkar said India's unity comes from its common constitutional values, not uniformity.

Westernisation of India

M.N. Srinivas coined the term "Westernisation" to describe changes that occurred in Indian society and culture due to British rule. It includes changes in technology, clothing, English education, food habits, and values like democracy and equality.

Today, Western influence continues through Hollywood, social media, fast food, corporate culture, and global brands. While this brings modernity, it also raises concerns about loss of traditional culture — this tension is called cultural conflict or identity crisis.

Unit III · 6 Hours

Social Stratification

🏗️ 3.1 What is Social Stratification?

📖 Definition Social Stratification means the division of society into different layers or ranks (called strata), where some groups have more power, wealth, status, or privileges than others. It is like a ladder — some people are at the top, some in the middle, and some at the bottom, and this ranking affects their life chances.

Stratification is universal (exists in every society), ancient (as old as human civilisation), and diverse (takes different forms — caste, class, race, gender). In India, the most important forms are the Caste System and the Class System.

📿 3.2 The Caste System

📖 Definition Caste is a hereditary, endogamous (marrying within the same group), and occupationally fixed social group. Your caste is determined by birth — you cannot change it. Caste prescribes what work you do, whom you marry, what you eat, and your social status.

Historical Origin of Caste

The caste system is rooted in the ancient Vedic concept of Varna (colour/class), which divided society into four groups:

VarnaTraditional RoleModern Equivalent
🙏 BrahminsPriests, scholars, teachersAcademic, religious professionals
⚔️ KshatriyasWarriors, rulers, kingsMilitary, police, administrators
💰 VaishyasMerchants, traders, farmersBusiness, commerce, banking
🔨 ShudrasArtisans, servants, labourersManual workers, craftspeople

Below the four Varnas were the Avarnas (untouchables / Dalits / Scheduled Castes), who were assigned the most stigmatised work — cleaning, handling dead bodies, leather work. They faced severe discrimination and were denied entry to temples, wells, and public spaces.

Characteristics of the Caste System

  • Hereditary – Caste is inherited at birth from parents; cannot be changed
  • Endogamy – One must marry within their own caste (inter-caste marriage was taboo)
  • Occupational rigidity – Each caste had a prescribed occupation passed down generations
  • Social restrictions – Rules about who can dine together, use the same water source, enter certain spaces
  • Ritual hierarchy – Purity and pollution rules — "upper" castes seen as ritually pure, "lower" castes as polluted
  • Corporate character – Caste groups act collectively, have their own panchayats (caste councils)

Problems Created by the Caste System

  • Untouchability — one of the most inhumane forms of discrimination
  • Denied education, economic opportunities, and dignity to lower castes for centuries
  • Caste-based violence (atrocities against Dalits) continues even today
  • Caste-based politics — parties seek caste vote banks
  • Honor killings — when people marry outside their caste

Modern Changes in the Caste System

The caste system has weakened significantly due to:

  • Constitutional provisions abolishing untouchability (Article 17)
  • Reservation policy in education and government jobs
  • Urbanisation — city life reduces caste visibility
  • Education — educated people are more caste-neutral
  • Social reform movements — B.R. Ambedkar, Jyotirao Phule, E.V. Ramasamy (Periyar)
  • Inter-caste marriages (legally protected, incentivised by government)

The Class System

📖 Definition Social Class is a group of people who share a similar economic position — same level of income, wealth, occupation, and education. Unlike caste, class is NOT fixed by birth. You can move up or down (called social mobility).
💎 Upper Class

Top 1–2% of society. Owns businesses, factories, large land. "Old money" aristocrats + new-age billionaires (Ambani, Tata, etc.)

👔 Middle Class

Salaried professionals, small business owners, doctors, engineers, teachers. India's middle class is 300+ million — the backbone of consumer economy.

👷 Working Class

Manual labourers, factory workers, daily wage earners. Often poor, lack job security, depend on physical work.

😔 Underclass

Permanently poor, homeless, unemployed, or marginally employed. Lack access to basic services — food, shelter, healthcare.

🌍 3.3 Communities, Ethnic Groups & Weaker Sections

Communities in India

A community is a group of people who share a common identity, interests, or location. India has thousands of communities defined by caste, religion, language, tribe, region, and occupation.

Ethnic Groups

📖 Definition An ethnic group is a group of people who share a common racial, cultural, linguistic, or ancestral heritage. They identify with each other as different from other groups. India has hundreds of ethnic groups — Marathas, Rajputs, Nagas, Bodos, Gujjars, Santals, etc.

Weaker Sections & Minorities

The Indian Constitution recognises certain groups as needing special protection due to historical discrimination or numerical disadvantage:

📋 Scheduled Castes (SC)

Castes listed in the Constitution's Schedule — historically "untouchables" (Dalits). ~16.6% of India's population. Entitled to reservations in education and government jobs.

🌿 Scheduled Tribes (ST)

Indigenous tribal communities — Adivasis. ~8.6% of population. Include Gonds, Santhals, Bhils, Mundas. Entitled to reservations and special rights over forests and land.

📜 Other Backward Classes (OBC)

Socially and educationally backward classes (other than SCs and STs). Mandal Commission (1980) identified 3,743 OBC communities — about 52% of India's population.

🕌 Minorities

Religious minorities (Muslims, Christians, Sikhs, Buddhists, Jains, Zoroastrians) and linguistic minorities protected under Articles 29 and 30.

Unit IV · 6 Hours

Socio-Economic Problems of India

🔍 4.1 Overview

India, despite being one of the world's fastest-growing economies, faces serious social and economic problems. These problems are inter-linked — poverty causes illiteracy, illiteracy causes unemployment, unemployment causes poverty. Together they form a vicious cycle of deprivation.

😔 4.2 Poverty

📖 Definition Poverty is a condition in which people lack the financial resources to meet basic needs — food, shelter, clothing, healthcare, and education. India's poverty line is defined by a minimum level of calorie intake (2400 kcal/day in rural areas, 2100 in urban) plus basic non-food needs.

Types of Poverty

💸 Absolute Poverty

Inability to afford minimum necessities of life. Living below the poverty line (BPL). About 21% of India (273 million people) live in absolute poverty as of recent estimates.

📊 Relative Poverty

Being poor compared to the average standard of living in society. Someone with ₹10,000/month may be "poor" in Mumbai but comfortable in rural Bihar.

Causes of Poverty in India

  • Rapid population growth — resources grow slower than people
  • Low agricultural productivity — 50% of workforce earns little from farming
  • Unemployment and underemployment
  • Unequal distribution of wealth — top 10% own 77% of national wealth
  • Illiteracy — limits access to good jobs
  • Casteism — Dalits and tribals face additional discrimination in employment
  • Lack of social security — no universal pension, healthcare, or unemployment benefit
  • Corruption — subsidies and welfare funds do not reach the poor

Government Anti-Poverty Programs

  • MGNREGA – 100 days of guaranteed wage employment for rural households
  • PM Awas Yojana – Housing for the poor
  • PM Jan Dhan Yojana – Financial inclusion — bank accounts for all
  • Public Distribution System (PDS) – Subsidised food grains for BPL families
  • PM Kisan Samman Nidhi – Direct income support to farmers

📚 4.3 Illiteracy

📖 Definition Illiteracy means inability to read and write. India's literacy rate as per Census 2011 is 74.04% (males 82.14%, females 65.46%). About 300 million Indians are illiterate — the largest illiterate population in the world.

Causes of Illiteracy

  • Poverty — poor families cannot afford books, uniforms, or keep children out of work
  • Child labour — children work instead of going to school
  • Gender discrimination — girls are kept home, married early
  • Lack of schools in rural/tribal areas
  • Poor quality of teaching and infrastructure
  • Dropout rates — many students leave school early

Key Initiatives

  • Right to Education Act (2009) – Free and compulsory education for children 6–14 years
  • Sarva Shiksha Abhiyan – Universal elementary education campaign
  • Mid-Day Meal Scheme – Encourages attendance with free meals
  • National Literacy Mission (Saakshar Bharat) – Adult literacy programs

💼 4.4 Unemployment

📖 Definition Unemployment means a situation where people who are willing and able to work cannot find work. India's unemployment rate fluctuates between 6–8% officially, but real unemployment (including disguised and underemployment) is much higher.
🌾 Disguised Unemployment

More people working on a job than required. Removing some would not reduce output. Common in Indian agriculture.

🔄 Seasonal Unemployment

Occurs only in certain seasons. Farmers unemployed in off-season. Construction workers idle during monsoon.

🎓 Educated Unemployment

Graduates and postgraduates unable to find suitable work. India produces 6+ million graduates annually; jobs don't match supply.

🏙️ Urban Unemployment

In cities, structural mismatch between skills people have and skills industries need. Also cyclical due to economic slowdowns.

🏠 4.5 Housing, Child Labour & Migration

Housing Problem

India faces a housing shortage of about 18.78 million units (mostly for urban poor). Rapid urbanisation has led to growth of slums. About 65 million Indians live in slums — Dharavi in Mumbai is Asia's largest slum. Problems include overcrowding, lack of sanitation, poor ventilation, and tenure insecurity.

Child Labour

📖 Definition Child Labour is when children under 14 years of age are engaged in work that deprives them of childhood, education, and physical/mental development. India has about 10.1 million child labourers (Census 2011), though the number is declining.
  • Causes: Poverty, lack of education, social norms, demand for cheap labour
  • Found in: carpet weaving, brick kilns, restaurants, agriculture, domestic work, match factories
  • Law: Child Labour (Prohibition and Regulation) Act, 1986 (amended 2016) — prohibits work for under-14s in all occupations, under-18s in hazardous industries
  • Government initiative: Pencil Portal — tracks child labour cases

Migration

Migration is the movement of people from one place to another for work, better living, education, or safety. India sees massive rural-to-urban migration.

🔑 Types of Migration Internal Migration — within India (rural to urban, state to state). International Migration — Indians moving abroad (Indian diaspora: 30+ million). Forced Migration — due to conflicts, disasters (Project Affected People).

Effects of Migration: Positive — remittances, reduced rural poverty, urbanisation. Negative — brain drain, family separation, overcrowding in cities, loss of social support.

💣 4.7 Insurgency & Terrorism

Insurgency

Insurgency is an armed rebellion against a government by a group that seeks political change. In India, major insurgency movements include:

  • Naxalism / Maoism – Armed movement in central-eastern India (Chhattisgarh, Jharkhand, Odisha) based on communist ideology; fight for tribal land rights
  • Northeastern Insurgency – Groups like ULFA (Assam), NSCN (Nagaland) demanding autonomy or independence
  • Kashmiri Militancy – Separatist movement in Jammu & Kashmir; some groups backed by foreign actors

Terrorism

Terrorism is the use of violence and intimidation against civilians to achieve political, religious, or ideological goals. India faces both domestic and cross-border terrorism. Key laws: UAPA (Unlawful Activities Prevention Act), NIA Act (National Investigation Agency).

⚖️ 4.8 Crime

Crime is any act that violates a law and is punishable by the state. India faces serious crime challenges:

👶 Crimes Against Children

Child trafficking, child sexual abuse, child labour. POCSO Act (2012) specifically protects children from sexual offences.

👩 Crimes Against Women

Rape, domestic violence, dowry death, acid attacks, harassment. National Crime Records Bureau (NCRB) data shows rising crime against women.

💻 Cybercrime

Online fraud, hacking, identity theft, cyberbullying. Fastest growing crime category in India. IT Act, 2000 governs this.

💰 White Collar Crime

Committed by educated, high-status individuals in professional settings — corruption, tax evasion, money laundering, bank fraud.

👴 4.9 Social Destitution, Beggary & Aged Population

Social Destitution & Beggary

Destitution refers to extreme poverty — lacking even the most basic necessities. Beggars are people who rely on public charity for survival. India has about 4 lakh beggars (Census 2011). Causes include mental illness, disability, poverty, loss of family support, and addiction.

Aged Population

India's elderly (60+) population is growing rapidly — currently about 138 million, expected to reach 300 million by 2050. Problems faced by elderly Indians:

  • Neglect by families — nuclear family trend means less care for parents
  • Lack of pension for those in informal sector
  • Rising healthcare costs
  • Loneliness and mental health issues
  • Elder abuse — physical, emotional, financial

Key law: Maintenance and Welfare of Parents and Senior Citizens Act, 2007 — legally obligates children to support parents.

👦 4.10 Juvenile Delinquency

📖 Definition Juvenile Delinquency refers to antisocial or criminal behaviour by individuals under 18 years of age. Examples include theft, vandalism, substance abuse, truancy, and violence. These are handled under the Juvenile Justice (Care and Protection of Children) Act, 2015.

Causes of Juvenile Delinquency

  • Broken or dysfunctional family
  • Poverty and lack of resources
  • Bad peer influence
  • Substance abuse (drugs, alcohol)
  • Lack of education and constructive activity
  • Social media influence and exposure to violence
  • Child abuse or neglect history

👨‍👩‍👧 4.11 Problems in Family Life

The Indian family is under transformation and stress. Major problems include:

💔 Domestic Violence

Physical, emotional, sexual, or economic abuse within the home. Protected by Domestic Violence Act, 2005. Women are primary victims.

💍 Dowry Issues

Illegal demanding of gifts/money from bride's family. Leads to domestic violence, female foeticide. Dowry Prohibition Act, 1961.

📋 Divorce & Single Parents

Rising divorce rates (especially in cities) creates financial and emotional burden, especially on women raising children alone.

👴 Generation Gap

Differences in values, lifestyle, and expectations between parents (traditional) and children (modern) cause family conflict and communication breakdown.

Project Affected People (PAPs)

📖 Definition Project Affected People (PAPs) are individuals or communities displaced due to large development projects — dams, highways, industries, mines, SEZs. They lose their land, homes, and livelihoods. India's big dams and projects have displaced over 50 million people since independence, mostly tribals and poor farmers.

Key law: Right to Fair Compensation and Transparency in Land Acquisition, Rehabilitation and Resettlement Act, 2013 — ensures PAPs receive proper compensation and rehabilitation.

Introduction to Human Behaviour

🧠 5.1 Overview of Human Behaviour

📖 Definition Human behaviour refers to everything that humans do — the actions they take, the thoughts they have, the feelings they experience, and how they interact with others and their environment. It includes visible actions (talking, working, fighting) as well as invisible ones (thinking, feeling, deciding).

Think of human behaviour as the output of a very complex machine (the human mind + body). The input is the environment, biology, upbringing, and experiences. The machine processes these, and the output is behaviour — what we say, do, and feel.

"Behaviour is the mirror in which everyone shows their image." — Johann Wolfgang von Goethe

What does Human Behaviour include?

🎬 Actions / Overt Behaviour

What you can directly observe — walking, speaking, eating, working, smiling, crying. These are external, visible behaviours.

💭 Mental / Covert Behaviour

What happens inside the mind — thinking, imagining, dreaming, feeling emotions, making decisions. Not directly observable but can be inferred.

🤝 Social Behaviour

How you interact with others — cooperation, competition, leadership, aggression, helping. Shaped by cultural norms and social roles.

🧬 Biological Behaviour

Behaviours driven by biological needs — eating, sleeping, reproducing, fighting or fleeing danger. Instinctive and partly genetic.

Nature of Human Behaviour — Key Characteristics

  1. Purposeful – Most human behaviour is goal-directed. People act to achieve something — food, money, love, status, safety
  2. Motivated – Behaviour is driven by needs, desires, and goals (see Maslow's Hierarchy below)
  3. Learned – Much of our behaviour is learned through experience, observation, and conditioning. This is called socialisation
  4. Variable – Human behaviour varies greatly between individuals, cultures, and situations. No two people behave exactly alike
  5. Adaptive – Humans change their behaviour to fit their environment — wear warm clothes in winter, speak formally in interviews
  6. Complex – Behaviour is influenced by multiple factors simultaneously (biology, mind, society, culture)
  7. Dynamic – Behaviour changes over time — a child's behaviour differs from a teenager's and an adult's
  8. Influenced by emotions – Emotions (fear, joy, anger, sadness) powerfully shape what we do

🎯 5.2 Importance of Studying Human Behaviour

Understanding human behaviour is not just academic — it is essential for almost every professional and personal aspect of life. Here's why it matters:

For Professionals and Organizations

  • Engineering & Technology – Engineers who understand human behaviour design better user-friendly products, safer workplaces, and more efficient systems (human factors engineering)
  • Management – Understanding employee motivation, leadership styles, team dynamics, and organisational culture helps managers lead effectively
  • Healthcare – Doctors and nurses who understand patient behaviour can improve compliance, reduce stress, and provide better care
  • Education – Teachers who understand learning behaviour can teach more effectively and support diverse students
  • Social Work – Understanding the behaviour of marginalised groups helps in better policy design and support programs
  • Law enforcement – Police and legal professionals use knowledge of criminal behaviour for investigation and rehabilitation
  • Marketing – Consumer behaviour knowledge helps design products and campaigns that people actually want

For Society and Personal Life

  • Helps understand why conflicts happen and how to resolve them peacefully
  • Helps identify and address social problems like addiction, violence, and delinquency
  • Promotes empathy — understanding why others behave as they do
  • Improves self-awareness — understanding our own motivations and biases
  • Helps in building better relationships — at home, work, and in community
  • Essential for mental health — understanding behaviour helps in therapy and counselling
💡 Application in Engineering Context Engineers must understand human behaviour for ergonomics (designing tools and machines that fit how humans naturally work), safety management (predicting risky behaviour to prevent accidents), project management (handling team dynamics, motivation, conflict), and community development (understanding how communities will respond to new infrastructure).

🔩 5.3 Determinants of Human Behaviour

Human behaviour is not random — it is shaped by many forces. These forces are called determinants. They can be grouped into three broad categories:

1. Biological / Hereditary Factors

📖 Nature vs Nurture The long-standing debate: is our behaviour due to nature (genes, biology) or nurture (environment, upbringing)? Modern science says BOTH interact to shape us (called gene-environment interaction).
🧬 Genetics

Genes influence intelligence, personality traits, temperament, risk of mental illness, and certain behavioural tendencies. E.g., identical twins raised apart still show similar personality traits.

🧠 Brain & Nervous System

Different brain regions control different behaviours. Prefrontal cortex = reasoning and decision making. Amygdala = fear and emotion. Damage to these areas changes behaviour dramatically.

⚗️ Hormones

Chemical messengers that affect mood and behaviour. Testosterone linked to aggression. Serotonin linked to happiness. Cortisol is the stress hormone. Hormonal imbalances cause depression, anxiety, aggression.

💪 Physical Health & Instincts

Physical well-being affects behaviour — pain, hunger, illness alter mood and actions. Basic instincts (survival, reproduction) drive fundamental behaviours.

2. Psychological Factors

🔑 Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs Abraham Maslow proposed that human behaviour is motivated by a pyramid of needs — from basic physical needs at the bottom to self-actualisation at the top. People first satisfy lower needs before pursuing higher ones.
LevelNeedExamples
5 (Top) 🌟Self-ActualisationCreativity, achieving full potential, purpose
4 🏆Esteem NeedsRespect, recognition, confidence, achievement
3 ❤️Love & BelongingFriendship, family, intimacy, sense of belonging
2 🛡️Safety NeedsSecurity, employment, health, property, stability

Other psychological factors that determine behaviour:

  • Perception – How we interpret the world around us. Two people see the same event differently based on their past experiences and beliefs
  • Attitude – Positive or negative evaluation of people, objects, or ideas. Attitudes influence behaviour (though not always directly)
  • Motivation – The driving force that pushes us to act. Intrinsic motivation (internal desire) vs extrinsic motivation (external rewards)
  • Personality – Consistent patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that make each person unique. Introverts vs extroverts behave very differently
  • Emotions – Fear, anger, joy, sadness, surprise, disgust — these "primary emotions" powerfully influence decisions and actions
  • Learning and memory – Past experiences stored in memory shape how we react to new situations
  • Intelligence – Problem-solving ability influences how people handle challenges and make decisions
  • Mental health – Depression, anxiety, schizophrenia, and other disorders profoundly alter behaviour

3. Environmental / Social Factors

👨‍👩‍👧 Family

The first and most powerful socialising agent. Parenting style (authoritative, authoritarian, permissive, neglectful) shapes personality, values, and social skills for life.

🏫 Education

Schools teach not just knowledge but social norms, discipline, cooperation, and values. The "hidden curriculum" shapes behaviour beyond what is formally taught.

👯 Peer Group

Friends and same-age groups are especially powerful during adolescence. Peer pressure can lead to both positive (academic excellence) and negative (substance abuse) behaviour.

🎭 Culture & Society

Cultural norms, values, and social roles prescribe what behaviour is acceptable. Indian culture values collective family decisions; Western culture values individual autonomy.

📺 Media

Television, internet, social media, news, and advertising influence attitudes, role models, and expectations. Social media especially shapes youth behaviour today.

💰 Economic Conditions

Poverty, unemployment, and financial stress cause anxiety, aggression, and desperation. Wealth gives access to opportunities that shape behaviour and aspirations.

🌍 Physical Environment

Climate, geography, urban vs rural setting, noise, crowding — all affect mood and behaviour. Extreme heat increases aggression; peaceful natural settings reduce stress.

🏛️ Political & Legal System

Laws, governance, and political stability (or instability) shape social behaviour. Rule of law encourages prosocial behaviour; weak institutions encourage corruption.

Summary: A Holistic Model of Behaviour Determinants

🌟 Big Picture Human behaviour is like an equation:
Behaviour = f (Biology + Psychology + Environment + Culture + Situation)

No single factor explains everything. The same person may behave differently at home vs office, in anger vs happiness, in childhood vs adulthood. This interactionist perspective is what modern psychology and sociology both support.

Individual Differences in Behaviour

Even with the same environment and upbringing, no two people behave identically. Individual differences arise from:

  • Personality type – Big Five personality traits: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism (OCEAN)
  • Intelligence (IQ) – Multiple intelligences: logical, linguistic, musical, spatial, bodily-kinesthetic, interpersonal, intrapersonal (Howard Gardner)
  • Emotional Intelligence (EQ) – Ability to understand and manage one's own emotions and empathise with others. Often more predictive of success than IQ
  • Locus of Control – Internals believe they control outcomes; externals believe fate/luck controls outcomes. Internals tend to be more proactive
  • Self-concept and self-esteem – How you see yourself and how good you feel about yourself fundamentally affects behaviour and achievement
